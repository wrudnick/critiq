Neon Cathedral

Lana Mori

By Simone Park · February 8, 2026 · Glass Bell Records

9.2
Best New Music

Lana Mori has been building toward something enormous for three albums, and on Neon Cathedral she finally arrives. The Japanese-American songwriter's fourth record is an art-pop landmark — eleven tracks that move between orchestral grandeur and whispered synth confessionals with the structural ambition of a gothic revival and the emotional directness of a voice note sent at 2 a.m.

The title track opens the album with a four-minute overture: strings swelling into a wall of distorted bass, Mori's voice floating above it all like a bird circling a burning building. It's dramatic in the way that the best pop music is dramatic — unashamed, almost reckless in its commitment to feeling. By the time the first chorus hits, you understand the title. This is music that occupies a vast interior space, light pouring through colored glass.

"Paper Gods" strips everything back to piano and breath. Mori's lyrics here are her sharpest — "I built you a temple out of things I couldn't say / and you used it as a parking lot" — and the production knows to get out of the way. It's the album's quietest moment and its most devastating.

The back half of Neon Cathedral takes risks that mostly pay off. "Hologram Bride" is a seven-minute electronic suite that sounds like Björk producing a Motown record. "Stained" layers gospel harmonies over industrial percussion. "Exit Through the Gift Shop" closes the album with an a cappella arrangement that somehow manages to be both a joke and a genuine emotional gut-punch.

If there's a criticism, it's that "Votive" and "Transept" — positioned back to back in the album's middle — share a tempo and mood that briefly stalls the momentum. But this is a minor structural complaint about an album that otherwise moves with extraordinary purpose. Neon Cathedral is Lana Mori's masterpiece, and one of the best pop albums in recent memory.
