Dirt Church

Jolene Wilder

By Hank Reeves · February 5, 2026 · Saddle Creek

7.6

Jolene Wilder's debut has the confidence of a fifth album. The Oklahoma singer-songwriter walks into Dirt Church with a voice that sounds like it's been aged in oak and a collection of songs that treat country music's conventions with equal parts reverence and irreverence. She knows the rules well enough to know which ones are worth breaking.

"Baptism by Gravel" opens the record with a fiddle drone and Wilder's unaccompanied voice before the band crashes in like a screen door in a storm. It's a mission statement: this is country music, but the church is outdoors, the pews are pickup truck tailgates, and the sermon is about surviving your twenties in a town with one stoplight.

The songwriting is sharp throughout. "Two-Lane Therapy" turns a long drive into a meditation on avoidance. "Sunday Best" is a devastating portrait of a woman who dresses up for a church she stopped believing in, held together by a pedal steel part so mournful it practically weeps. Wilder has a gift for the specific detail that unlocks a universal feeling — the "coffee ring on the divorce papers" in "Settlement" is the kind of image that stops you cold.

Where Dirt Church stumbles is in its middle stretch, where three mid-tempo ballads in a row drain some of the energy that the opening tracks build. "Fence Line" and "Hard Water" are well-crafted but interchangeable, and the album could have used either another barnburner or a stranger detour to break up the texture. But the closing track, "Amen," is worth the wait — a six-minute slow burn that earns every second of its final, cathartic key change.

Wilder is a talent worth watching. Dirt Church isn't perfect, but it's the most promising country debut since Waxahatchee decided to go twang.
